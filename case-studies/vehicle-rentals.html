Vehicle Rentals: Support Reimagined

"We have a problem and we don't know where to start. Can you help?" — My favorite kind of problem.

RoleUX Designer
CompanyHome Depot
TypeEnterprise · Field Operations
Outcome38 min saved · +48 rent-ready days · $6K revenue/vehicle/year

Scenario

This story starts with an email: "We have a problem and we don't know where to start. Can you help?" My favorite kind of problem.

Home Depot's entire vehicle rental fleet is managed by a third party, Element. The problem involved two distinct user groups: Home Depot in-store associates and Element field technicians. Due to COVID-related supply chain strain, reduced manpower, and increased rental demand, inefficiencies in fleet maintenance had become a real business problem.

Element wanted to increase user satisfaction to handle the planned growth of the vehicle rental fleet. As the UX Designer, I was responsible for scoping the problem, facilitating research, and creating the digital assets needed to solve it.

Research

Surveys

To gather feedback from Rental Center Associates about their experience with Element, we set up a survey in Microsoft Forms and distributed it to 300 stores. Ninety associates responded. The survey used closed questions and Likert scales, with follow-up video interviews conducted to add depth.

Key Research Finding

Associates mostly liked working with Element. The method of communication was the most frequently reported problem.

Associates expected a digital method of communication — not a phone tree. They told us exactly what was frustrating them and what they needed to fix it.

Key findings from the readout: associates liked Element as a partner, but the process of reaching them was broken. Five respondents were also interviewed via video conference for additional context and detail.

Design

Sketches

I start almost every design process with low-fidelity sketches on paper to capture the end-to-end experience and surface opportunities that might not be obvious when looking at individual screens. Storyboards were created to help stakeholders see the scope of work in context. Rough sketches also introduced the idea of incorporating tablets into the overall solution and helped connect individual pain points to broader initiatives like Mobile Contracts.

Key learnings from sketches: they reveal patterns that users already know and like. They also identify missing information — license plate numbers were identified as a critical gap through this process.

UI Design

Final screens were designed in Figma following the Home Depot style guide using the ANT design system. Key design decisions: responsiveness (research showed users expected flexibility across devices), familiarity (the layout closely resembled an existing application that had already tested well), and new capabilities (long-requested features like asset images and the ability to transfer vehicles to any Home Depot store or holding facility).

High-fidelity prototypes were used to validate designs through moderated usability tests. One lesson: small, subtle UI elements like blue text links for editing and reporting issues created more confusion than they solved — they were cut before launch.

Outcomes & Impact

38 minDecrease in time on task per maintenance instance
+48More rent-ready days per vehicle per year
$6,000Increase in revenue per vehicle, per year
<90 daysFrom inception to people in the field — even as an outside-roadmap initiative

What We Learned

This initiative showed that even large companies can move fast when given space to do so. Because this project lived outside the normal product roadmap, there was freedom to solve the big problem without getting bogged down in competing priorities. Insisting on baseline metrics before starting gave the team something to rally around — and revealed opportunities in unexpected areas.
