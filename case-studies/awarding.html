Winning Buy-In With User Evidence: How 'Awarding' Boosted Retention and Engagement by 65%

"I don't know what it wants me to do." — that single user quote kicked off a complete redesign of Arkestro's most business-critical workflow.

RoleHead of UX
CompanyArkestro
TypeB2B SaaS · Enterprise Procurement
OutcomeEngagement 55%→74% · Retention +65%

Situation

The Awarding experience allows buyers to select which supplier receives a contract. Despite being a critical step in the procurement workflow, the feature had low adoption and frequent user confusion. Support tickets were high and the enablement team was spending significant time helping users through this part of the process.

The interface had originally been built quickly before the company had dedicated UX resources. The page lacked a clear information hierarchy, the primary call to action was vague, and users often didn't trust the returned results. Buyers also had little visibility into what would happen after awarding a supplier, creating hesitation during a high-stakes decision.

Because the workflow didn't align with how buyers actually worked, many continued managing decisions in Excel-based tools — creating duplicate work by entering the same information in multiple places.

Task

My goal was to identify the sources of friction and redesign the awarding workflow so buyers could confidently complete the process within the platform. The initiative aimed to: increase platform adoption, increase repeat usage, drive more spend through the platform, reduce support sessions, and enable split awarding.

Research

I conducted user interviews and workflow reviews to identify friction points and establish baseline metrics. I recreated the existing interface in Figma and walked through the workflow with users and stakeholders, helping pinpoint where confusion occurred and align on targeted improvements.

Key Research Finding

The experience caused duplicate work, lacked a clear call to action, and often left users uncertain about what the page expected them to do.

As a result, many buyers avoided the platform or completed parts of the process elsewhere — most often in spreadsheets.

A cross-functional design studio was conducted with PM, UX, Devs, and end users to identify gaps in the existing experience and promote shared ownership of the solution.

Design Process

Low-Fidelity Mockups

Low-fidelity mockups validated the problem with internal stakeholders first. A key learning: engineers had traditionally had heavy influence on product design based on what was easiest to build. I introduced more design crits and feedback sessions to build trust while still advocating for users. The result was a better working relationship and a team that flagged gaps earlier.

High-Fidelity Mockups

High-fidelity mockups were tested with internal and external users, featuring: improved information architecture with clearer hierarchy, improved calls to action so users knew exactly what to do next, and award distribution infographics showing which suppliers were getting how much spend.

Design Tradeoffs

This work required balancing two very different user groups — light users who needed guidance and reassurance, and power users who valued speed. Because awarding is a high-stakes decision, I prioritized clarity and confidence over raw efficiency.

I focused on surfacing high-signal insights rather than exposing all underlying data. Moving to a more guided flow reduced flexibility somewhat, but significantly improved completion rates. New features like split awarding added complexity, so I used progressive disclosure to keep the experience manageable.

Rollout

After high-fidelity designs were implemented, I released the new awarding workflow to a small beta group to monitor adoption and gather feedback. Early signals were encouraging — more buyers were completing awards directly in the platform instead of relying on Excel workflows. Based on feedback, I made small adjustments to labeling and guidance before rolling out more broadly.

Outcomes & Impact

74%Engagement rate — up from a baseline of 55%, within the first quarter after rollout
+65%Increase in retention — users choosing to complete awarding in-platform instead of external tools
↑ MLMore awarding events fed the platform's machine learning models, making recommendations more accurate and actionable

What We Learned

Better metrics gathering was needed to show quantitative changes after feature rollout. We identified new metrics for future initiatives: average time to complete an award, number of support tickets, and amount of spend through the platform. We also identified gaps in how Product, UX, and Engineering worked together — and immediately set about fixing them with color-coded Figma files and pre-refinement meetings.
